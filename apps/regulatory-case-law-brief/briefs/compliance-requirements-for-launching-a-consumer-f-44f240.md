# Compliance Brief — Compliance requirements for launching a consumer fintech lending product in Texas

**Jurisdiction:** State of Texas (state licensing regime administered by the Office of Consumer Credit Commissioner), plus applicable federal consumer-finance law (CFPB/Dodd-Frank, TILA, ECOA, MLA)
_Research summary, not legal advice._

## Summary
RESEARCH, NOT LEGAL ADVICE. Launching a consumer fintech lending product in Texas means operating at the intersection of a Texas state licensing regime and federal consumer-protection law.

STATE (Texas): Consumer loans are governed by Texas Finance Code (TFC) Title 4. The two state-law paths a fintech most often uses are: (1) TFC Chapter 342 'Consumer Loans' — a regulated lender license issued by the Office of Consumer Credit Commissioner (OCCC) for small-dollar/installment loans, with statutory rate ceilings and licensing/exam obligations; and (2) the Chapter 393 'Credit Services Organizations' (CSO) / 'credit access business' (CAB) model used by many online/bank-partnerhip fintechs, where the fintech arranges a loan made by a third-party (often out-of-state) bank and charges its own fees. Both require OCCC registration/licensing, disclosures, recordkeeping, and annual reporting; the OCCC administers exams and brings administrative enforcement (injunctions, revocations, penalties) for violations. As of 2025, the OCCC is migrating Chapter 342 regulated-lender licensing to the Nationwide Multistate Licensing System (NMLS) under TFC §14.109/§342.551.

FEDERAL: Any consumer lender — including a fintech — must comply with federal law enforced by the CFPB: Truth in Lending/Regulation Z (12 CFR 1026, cost disclosures, APR), Equal Credit Opportunity Act/Regulation B (12 CFR 1002, no discrimination, adverse-action notices), EFTA/Regulation E for electronic payments, Gramm-Leach-Bliley privacy, the Military Lending Act (36% MAPR cap for servicemembers and dependents), and Dodd-Frank's UDAAP prohibition (unfair, deceptive, or abusive acts/practices). CFPB supervision reaches nonbank larger participants.

ENFORCEMENT & CASE LAW: The CFPB's two ACE Cash Express (Irving, TX) actions are the headline precedents — a 2014 $10M consent order for illegal debt-collection/cycle-of-debt UDAAP, and a 2022 suit (N.D. Tex., 3:22-cv-01494) alleging concealment of free repayment plans, which the Bureau voluntarily dismissed with prejudice on April 30, 2025. The OCCC's own enforcement docket shows steady use of injunctions and license revocations against regulated lenders and CABs. The 2025 FirstCash Military Lending Act settlement shows MLA risk for pawn/payday-style products.

RECENT/PENDING (last ~24 months): Texas OCCC NMLS migration rules adopted Dec. 26, 2025 (7 TAC §§83.301-83.404); OCCC CAB licensing & recordkeeping rule amendments out for precomment (draft posted July 15, 2026); CFPB Personal Financial Data Rights / open-banking rule finalized Oct. 22, 2024 (Section 1033); CFPB digital-payment-apps larger-participant rule finalized Nov. 21, 2024; CFPB BNPL interpretive rule (Regulation Z) issued May 31, 2024; and in 2025 the CFPB rescinded/withdrew many rules and guidance and several Chopra-era rules were nullified by Congressional Review Act joint resolutions. Two named Texas consumer-lending cases the user asked about — Looney v. Cash America and Davis v. ACE Cash Express — were searched for but no primary court opinion was located in this corpus (see key_cases notes).

BOTTOM LINE FOR A FOUNDER: Before launch you need (a) the right Texas OCCC license or CAB registration, (b) federal compliance rails for TILA/ECOA/MLA/UDAAP/privacy, (c) APR/rate-ceiling math that fits the TFC chapter you elect, and (d) a watch on the moving 2024-2026 CFPB and OCCC rule changes. This is a compliance brief, not a legal opinion; engage Texas consumer-finance counsel before going live.

## Applicable regulations
- **Texas Finance Code Chapter 342 — Consumer Loans (Regulated Lender License)** (Texas (administered by OCCC)) — A fintech making consumer small-dollar or installment loans to Texas borrowers generally needs an OCCC regulated lender license under Chapter 342 (see also 7 TAC Chapter 83). Licensees must meet application/character/financial-responsibility standards, use NMLS for application and renewal (migration in progress, per Dec. 26 2025 adopted rules), keep records, file annual reports, submit to OCCC examination, and comply with Chapter 342's statutory rate ceilings and contract requirements. Note: the retrieved statute page extracted only navigation chrome; section-level text (rate caps, fees) was not captured — confirm exact ceilings in the statute text with counsel.
- **Texas Finance Code Chapter 393 — Credit Services Organizations (CSO) / Credit Access Businesses (CAB)** (Texas (administered by OCCC; CSO registration with Texas Secretary of State)) — Fintechs using the bank-partnership/CAB model register as a credit services organization / credit access business. A CAB contract must contain specified disclosures (incl. a no-prepayment-penalty statement per §393.201(c)), must not obtain credit for a consumer within seven days of a prior CAB loan being paid off (§393.308-type rollover restriction), keep records, and comply with OCCC CAB rules; municipalities may impose additional CAB ordinances that Chapter 393 does not preempt (§393.004). The OCCC is currently proposing CAB licensing & recordkeeping rule amendments (precomment draft posted July 15, 2026). Note: section-level statutory text beyond the HB 768 excerpts was not captured from the statute page — confirm exact §393.101-393.602 obligations in the statute.  [source](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/docs/FI/htm/FI.393.htm)
- **Texas Finance Code Chapter 305 — Penalties and Remedies (Usury)** (Texas) — Chapter 305 establishes civil penalties and remedies for usury and other Finance Code violations (e.g., forfeiture of interest, penalties) applicable to consumer lenders that exceed rate ceilings. A fintech must ensure its APR/fees do not breach the applicable ceiling under the TFC chapter it elects (Chapter 342 or 303/351). Note: section-level text was not captured from the page (navigation chrome only); confirm specific penalty amounts in the statute.
- **Texas Finance Code Chapter 303 — Optional Rate Ceilings** (Texas) — Chapter 303 provides optional rate-ceiling elections that certain consumer lenders may use; combined with Chapter 342's specific ceilings it determines the maximum lawful rate/charges. A fintech must compute its APR against the correct ceiling to avoid usury exposure under Chapters 305/351. Note: section-level ceiling figures were not captured from the page (navigation chrome only); confirm exact ceilings in the statute text.
- **Truth in Lending Act / Regulation Z (12 CFR Part 1026)** (Federal (CFPB-administered)) — A consumer lender must make clear, accurate cost disclosures before extending credit — amount financed, finance charge, APR, payment schedule — and provide periodic statements and billing-error rights for open-end credit; prohibitions on certain terms and practices. A May 31, 2024 CFPB interpretive rule clarified that BNPL/digital-user-account lenders are 'card issuers' subject to Regulation Z (periodic statements, billing disputes).  [source](https://law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/12/part-1026)
- **Equal Credit Opportunity Act / Regulation B (12 CFR Part 1002)** (Federal (CFPB-administered)) — A lender may not discriminate on a prohibited basis (race, color, religion, national origin, sex, marital status, age, receipt of public assistance, exercise of rights under the CCPA) in any aspect of a credit transaction; must provide adverse-action notices within the required timeframe (§1002.9), retain records (§1002.12), and may collect monitoring information (§1002.13). ECOA is a federal floor and does not preempt stricter Texas protections.  [source](https://ecfr.gov/current/title-12/chapter-X/part-1002)
- **Dodd-Frank Act §1031/§1036 — Unfair, Deceptive, or Abusive Acts or Practices (UDAAP)** (Federal (CFPB-administered; applies concurrently with Texas law)) — A fintech lender must not engage in unfair, deceptive, or abusive acts or practices in offering or servicing consumer credit. The CFPB's 2014 ACE Cash Express order is the canonical example: illegal debt-collection threats, harassment, and pressuring borrowers into a cycle of debt were held to be UDAAP violations, resulting in $5M consumer refunds and a $5M civil penalty. UDAAP is the CFPB's broadest enforcement hook and applies to all consumer-finance conduct.  [source](https://consumerfinance.gov/enforcement/actions)
- **Military Lending Act (10 U.S.C. §987) — 36% MAPR cap and disclosures for servicemembers** (Federal (CFPB/DOJ-enforced)) — For covered consumer credit extended to active-duty servicemembers and certain dependents, a lender must cap the military annual percentage rate (MAPR) at 36%, must not require arbitration or waivers of rights, and must make specified MLA disclosures. The 2025 FirstCash settlement (Fort Worth, TX pawn lender) shows the CFPB actively enforces the MLA and the 36% cap, including against Texas-based operators of pawn/title/payday-style products.
- **CFPB Personal Financial Data Rights Rule — Section 1033 (open banking / data portability)** (Federal (CFPB-administered)) — Covered financial providers (including fintech lenders that hold consumer financial data) must, at the consumer's request, make personal financial data available and transfer it to another provider for free; limit data use to the requested product purpose (ban on bait-and-switch data harvesting); provide revocation and deletion rights (access ends immediately on revocation; deletion default; access max one year absent reauthorization). Compliance is phased by institution size — largest by April 1, 2026; smallest by April 1, 2030. Relevant to fintech lenders that pull or hold consumer banking data for underwriting.
- **CFPB Larger-Participant / Nonbank Supervision Authority (12 U.S.C. §5514; Dodd-Frank Title X)** (Federal (CFPB)) — The CFPB may define 'larger participants' in consumer-finance markets and subject nonbank companies meeting the threshold to CFPB supervisory examinations (in addition to enforcement). For fintech lenders, relevant larger-participant markets include consumer reporting, debt collection, student loan servicing, international money transfers, automobile financing, and (as of Nov. 2024) digital payment apps (>50M transactions/yr). A fintech lender above a market threshold must expect CFPB exams, recordkeeping, and compliance-management-system scrutiny.  [source](https://consumerfinance.gov/compliance/guidance)

## Key case law
- **CFPB v. ACE Cash Express, Inc. (2014 consent order)** (CFPB (administrative consent order), 2014-07-10) — The CFPB found ACE (Irving, TX) engaged in unfair, deceptive, and abusive debt-collection and cycle-of-debt practices in violation of Dodd-Frank UDAAP; ordered $5M consumer refunds and a $5M civil penalty, and barred the abusive practices.  [source](https://consumerfinance.gov/archive/newsroom/cfpb-takes-action-against-ace-cash-express-for-pushing-payday-borrowers-into-cycle-of-debt)
- **CFPB v. Populus Financial Group, Inc. d/b/a ACE Cash Express, Inc. (N.D. Tex. 2022)** (U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Texas (3:22-cv-01494), 2022-07-12 (filed); dismissed 2025-04-30) — The CFPB alleged ACE concealed no-cost repayment plans and improperly withdrew funds (UDAAP under the CFPA); on April 30, 2025 the Bureau voluntarily dismissed the action with prejudice and the court terminated the case — a notable 2025 retreat from a Texas payday-lender UDAAP suit.  [source](https://consumerfinance.gov/enforcement/actions/populus-financial-group-inc-d-b-a-ace-cash-express-inc)
- **CFPB v. FirstCash, Inc. (Military Lending Act settlement)** (Federal court (stipulated final judgment entered), 2025-07-11 (settlement/judgment); suit originally filed 2021-11-12) — FirstCash (Fort Worth, TX pawn lender) made pawn loans to MLA-covered servicemembers exceeding the 36% MAPR cap and included prohibited arbitration clauses; ordered $5M redress and a $4M fine, and must offer an MLA-compliant product or use the MLA safe-harbor screening.
- **OCCC v. Cottonwood Financial Texas LLC (Credit Access Business enforcement)** (Texas Office of Consumer Credit Commissioner (administrative enforcement), 2025-02-27) — The OCCC issued a final injunction order against a registered Credit Access Business under TFC §14.202 — illustrating that the OCCC affirmatively enforces the CAB registration regime (not just licensed lenders), a direct compliance risk for fintech CABs.  [source](https://occc.texas.gov/publications/legal/enforcement-actions)
- **Looney v. Cash America International, Inc.**
- **Davis v. ACE Cash Express, Inc.**

## Recent changes
- OCCC adopted rules (7 TAC §§83.301-83.404) migrating Chapter 342 regulated-lender licensing from ALECS to the Nationwide Multistate Licensing System (NMLS), authorized under TFC §14.109 and §342.551; a majority of licensed regulated lenders completed the NMLS transition in 2025. Directly affects how a new fintech applies for and renews a Texas regulated lender license. (2025-12-26)  [source](https://sos.state.tx.us/texreg/archive/December262025/Adopted%20Rules/7.BANKING%20AND%20SECURITIES.html)
- OCCC released a precomment draft of Credit Access Business (CAB) Licensing & Recordkeeping rule amendments (informal precomments accepted through Aug. 5, 2026) — a pending rulemaking that would update CAB registration, recordkeeping, and (per the pawnshop parallel) likely data-security/breach requirements. Fintechs operating the CAB/bank-partnership model should track this rulemaking before it is finalized. (2026-07-15 (draft posted))  [source](https://occc.texas.gov/publications/legal/rules)
- CFPB finalized the Personal Financial Data Rights (open-banking) rule under Section 1033 (Oct. 22, 2024), requiring covered providers to make consumer financial data portable on request with privacy limits; compliance phased by size (largest by April 1, 2026; smallest by April 1, 2030). However, subsequent CFPB signals (Dec. 2025) indicated interim final rules on Section 1033 amid funding constraints, so the effective timeline may shift — monitor CFPB rulemaking status. (2024-10-22)
- CFPB finalized a larger-participant rule (Nov. 21, 2024) bringing federal supervision to the largest nonbank digital payment/wallet apps handling >50 million transactions/year (privacy, fraud, debanking). Relevant to fintech lenders that also operate payment/wallet rails. (2024-11-21)  [source](https://consumerfinance.gov/archive/newsroom/cfpb-finalizes-rule-on-federal-oversight-of-popular-digital-payment-apps-to-protect-personal-data-reduce-fraud-and-stop-illegal-debanking)
- CFPB issued an interpretive rule (May 31, 2024) clarifying that Buy Now, Pay Later / digital-user-account lenders are 'card issuers' subject to Regulation Z subpart B (periodic statements, billing-error/dispute rights). Fintechs offering BNPL-style consumer credit must build TILA/Reg Z compliance around these obligations. (2024-05-31)  [source](https://federalregister.gov/documents/2024/05/31/2024-11800/truth-in-lending-regulation-z-use-of-digital-user-accounts-to-access-buy-now-pay-later-loans)
- In 2025, the CFPB rescinded or withdrew many final rules, proposed rules, and guidance documents issued since 2011, and several rules finalized under Director Chopra were made ineffective by Congressional Review Act joint resolutions or vacated by a court. Most notably for this brief, the Bureau voluntarily dismissed its 2022 UDAAP suit against ACE Cash Express with prejudice on April 30, 2025, signaling a more constrained 2025 enforcement posture. A fintech should not read this as a deregulation of substantive TILA/ECOA/MLA duties, but should monitor which CFPB rules remain in effect. (2025 (through 2026-05-28))  [source](https://consumerfinance.gov/rules-policy)
- The CFPB reached a Military Lending Act settlement with FirstCash, Inc. (Fort Worth, TX) on July 11, 2025 — $5M redress and a $4M fine for making pawn loans to covered servicemembers exceeding the 36% MAPR cap and including prohibited arbitration clauses. Reinforces that the federal 36% MLA cap and disclosure requirements apply to Texas small-dollar/pawn/title/payday-style consumer credit and are actively enforced. (2025-07-11)
