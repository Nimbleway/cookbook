# Compliance Brief — Data privacy obligations for a US SaaS company serving EU customers

**Jurisdiction:** European Union (primary: GDPR, ePrivacy Directive, EU-US Data Privacy Framework, EU AI Act) and United States (federal: FTC Act Section 5; state: California CCPA/CPRA)
_Research summary, not legal advice._

## Summary
This is research, not legal advice; consult qualified counsel before acting. A US-based SaaS company that processes EU residents' personal data is pulled into EU data-protection law primarily through GDPR Article 3(2), which applies to a non-EU controller that offers goods/services to, or monitors the behaviour of, people in the Union. In practice that means the company must meet the full GDPR toolkit: a lawful basis and the processing principles (Art 5-6); transparency and the data-subject rights of access, rectification, erasure, portability and objection (Art 12-22); appointing an EU representative because it has no EU establishment (Art 27); written data-processing agreements with any sub-processors (Art 28); security of processing (Art 32); breach notification to a supervisory authority within 72 hours and to affected individuals (Art 33-34); and the Chapter V transfer regime. For the SaaS company's own tracking cookies and electronic messaging, the ePrivacy Directive (2002/58/EC) Art 5(3) requires informed consent before storing/accessing information on a user's terminal equipment. If the product uses AI and is placed on the EU market, the EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) adds tiered obligations: prohibited practices apply since 2 Feb 2025, general-purpose-AI model duties since 2 Aug 2025, and high-risk AI system duties from 2 Aug 2026. The hardest issue is Chapter V — getting EU personal data lawfully to US servers. There are two main routes. First, the EU-US Data Privacy Framework: the Commission's 10 July 2023 adequacy decision lets a US company receive EU data without additional safeguards IF it self-certifies with the Department of Commerce and commits to the DPF Principles (enforced by the FTC under Section 5); the decision relies on EO 14086's necessity/proportionality limits on US signals intelligence and a new Data Protection Review Court with binding remedial powers. Second, if the company cannot or will not join the DPF, it must use the 2021 Standard Contractual Clauses (Decision 2021/914) AND, per Schrems II, perform a transfer impact assessment of US law and add supplementary measures (e.g., strong encryption) — and suspend transfers if equivalent protection cannot be ensured. The Meta Ireland €1.2 billion fine (22 May 2023) shows the real-world cost of getting this wrong: the DPC found Meta infringed Art 46(1) by continuing EU-to-US transfers after Schrems II even with SCCs plus supplementary measures, ordered a transfer suspension and €1.2bn fine. On the US side, the FTC Act Section 5 (15 U.S.C. §§ 41-58) prohibits unfair or deceptive privacy/data-security practices and is the backstop for DPF commitments; and for US-resident data (e.g., California consumers) the CCPA/CPRA (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100 et seq.) requires, when acting as a service provider, a written contract limiting use to specified purposes and mandating the same level of privacy protection — conceptually overlapping with GDPR Art 28 processor duties. Binding vs. persuasive: the GDPR, ePrivacy Directive, AI Act, FTC Act and CCPA are binding statutes/regulations; Schrems II (CJEU Case C-311/18) is binding EU case law; the Meta Ireland decision is a binding supervisory-authority decision (affirmed via an EDPB Art 65 binding decision); the DPF adequacy decision and EO 14086 are binding instruments; FTC enforcement actions are agency orders. Gaps flagged: a pending 'Schrems III' challenge to the DPF adequacy decision (NOYB/Max Schrems have publicly signaled challenges) was NOT found in any retrieved primary court source (curia.europa.eu) after search — this is 'not found after search,' NOT 'confirmed none'; and the specific outcome of the DPF's first periodic review (announced to occur within a year of entry into force) was not retrieved.

## Applicable regulations
- **General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2016/679)** (European Union (extraterritorial application to non-EU controllers/processors via Art. 3(2))) — Art 3(2) extraterritorial scope: applies to a non-EU controller offering goods/services to or monitoring EU data subjects. Concrete obligations: lawful basis & principles (Art 5-6); transparency and data-subject rights — access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection (Art 12-22); appoint an EU representative if subject under Art 3(2) and no EU establishment (Art 27); written data-processing agreements with processors (Art 28); security of processing (Art 32); breach notification to supervisory authority within 72h and to affected individuals (Art 33-34); and Chapter V transfer rules requiring an adequacy decision, appropriate safeguards (e.g., SCCs), or a derogation before transferring personal data outside the EEA (Art 44-49).  [source](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-3-gdpr)
- **EU-US Data Privacy Framework (adequacy decision adopted 10 July 2023)** (European Union – United States (transatlantic transfers under GDPR Art. 45)) — A US SaaS company joins by self-certifying with the US Department of Commerce and committing to the DPF Principles (e.g., delete personal data when no longer necessary; ensure continuity of protection when sharing with third parties). Compliance with those commitments is enforced by the FTC under Section 5. The framework also relies on new US safeguards: intelligence access limited to what is necessary and proportionate, and a Data Protection Review Court (DPRC) that can adopt binding remedial measures (including data deletion) for EU-individual complaints. The framework is subject to periodic Commission review (first review within a year of entry into force).  [source](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/api/files/document/print/en/ip_23_3721/IP_23_3721_EN.pdf)
- **Standard Contractual Clauses — Commission Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914 of 4 June 2021** (European Union (transfers to third countries incl. the United States)) — The data exporter and importer execute the modular SCCs to provide 'appropriate safeguards' under Art 46(1). Per Schrems II, the exporter must conduct a transfer impact assessment of the third country's law (esp. government access) and add supplementary measures (e.g., encryption) where the SCCs cannot otherwise be honoured; if protection cannot be ensured, suspend/prohibit the transfer. Exporters must also inform data subjects of the transfer/safeguards under Art 13/14. SCCs also apply to non-EU-established controllers caught by Art 3(2).  [source](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/HTML?uri=CELEX%3A32021D0914)
- **ePrivacy Directive (Directive 2002/58/EC, as amended)** (European Union) — Art 5(3): storing information on, or gaining access to information already stored in, a user's terminal equipment (i.e., cookies and similar tracking technologies) is allowed only with the user's consent, after providing clear and comprehensive information about the purposes of processing. Art 5(1) requires confidentiality of communications. Consent must meet the GDPR standard (freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous).  [source](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/LexUriServ/LexUriServ.do?uri=CONSLEG%3A2002L0058%3A20091219%3AEN%3AHTML)
- **EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 of 13 June 2024)** (European Union) — Risk-based obligations for AI systems placed on the EU market: prohibited AI practices (Art 5) apply from 2 Feb 2025; obligations for providers of general-purpose AI (GPAI) models, governance and penalties apply from 2 Aug 2025; and high-risk AI system obligations (risk management, data governance, transparency, human oversight, logging) apply from 2 Aug 2026. The Act is without prejudice to GDPR data-protection law and is complementary to it.  [source](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT?uri=CELEX%3A32024R1689)
- **Federal Trade Commission Act, Section 5 (15 U.S.C. §§ 41-58)** (United States (federal)) — Prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in or affecting commerce; the FTC enforces companies' privacy and data-security representations and can seek monetary redress. A US SaaS company must not misrepresent its privacy/data practices, and its DPF self-certification commitments are enforceable by the FTC under Section 5.  [source](https://ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/statutes/federal-trade-commission-act)
- **California Consumer Privacy Act / California Privacy Rights Act (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100 et seq.)** (California, United States) — When a SaaS company acts as a service provider/processor, it must operate under a written contract with the business that: (1) specifies the personal information is disclosed only for limited and specified purposes; and (2) obligates the service provider to comply with the title and provide the same level of privacy protection. As a business collecting its own data it must give notice at collection, limit use to disclosed/compatible purposes, and keep retention reasonably necessary. These overlap conceptually with GDPR processor duties (Art 28).  [source](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayText.xhtml?division=3.&lawCode=CIV&part=4.&title=1.81.5)
- **Executive Order 14086 — Enhancing Safeguards for United States Signals Intelligence Activities (7 Oct 2022)** (United States (federal; prerequisite for EU-US DPF adequacy)) — Limits US signals-intelligence access to data to what is necessary to advance a validated intelligence priority and proportionate to that priority, with privacy/civil-liberties consideration for all persons regardless of nationality (Sec 2). Establishes a redress mechanism (Sec 3): qualifying complaints from a qualifying state (incl. EU individuals) are investigated by the Civil Liberties Protection Officer, with review by a Data Protection Review Court (DPRC) that can adopt binding remedial measures and order deletion. This is the US safeguard that underpins the DPF adequacy decision.  [source](https://federalregister.gov/documents/2022/10/14/2022-22531/enhancing-safeguards-for-united-states-signals-intelligence-activities)

## Key case law
- **Data Protection Commissioner v Facebook Ireland and Maximillian Schrems (Case C-311/18) ('Schrems II')** (Court of Justice of the European Union (Grand Chamber), 16 July 2020) — Invalidated Commission Decision 2016/1250 (the EU-US Privacy Shield adequacy decision) because US surveillance law was not proportionately limited and the Ombudsperson mechanism did not provide effective judicial redress; upheld the validity of the standard contractual clauses (Decision 2010/87) but held transfers under SCCs require a level of protection 'essentially equivalent' to EU law — exporters must assess the third country's law (including government access) and add supplementary measures, and supervisory authorities must suspend/prohibit transfers where SCCs cannot be honoured.  [source](https://curia.europa.eu/jcms/upload/docs/application/pdf/2020-07/cp200091en.pdf)
- **Data Protection Commission v Meta Platforms Ireland Limited (Meta Ireland €1.2 billion fine)** (Irish Data Protection Commission (lead supervisory authority), with EDPB Art. 65 binding decision (13 April 2023), 22 May 2023 (final decision adopted 12 May 2023; EDPB Art. 65 decision 13 April 2023)) — Meta Ireland was found to have infringed Article 46(1) GDPR by continuing EU-to-US transfers after Schrems II on the basis of the 2021 SCCs plus supplementary measures that the DPC found did not address the risks to data subjects' fundamental rights; ordered to suspend future transfers within 5 months, to bring processing into compliance with Chapter V within 6 months, and fined €1.2 billion — the largest GDPR fine to date.  [source](https://dataprotection.ie/en/news-media/press-releases/Data-Protection-Commission-announces-conclusion-of-inquiry-into-Meta-Ireland)
- **FTC v DoNotPay and others — 'Operation AI Comply' (deceptive AI claims sweep)** (U.S. Federal Trade Commission (administrative enforcement under FTC Act Section 5), 25 September 2024) — The FTC brought five law enforcement actions against companies using AI hype or selling AI tools in deceptive/unfair ways, charging violations of Section 5 of the FTC Act; establishes that there is 'no AI exemption from the laws on the books' and that deceptive AI marketing and AI tools that enable deception are actionable — relevant to a SaaS company marketing AI features to EU/US customers.  [source](https://ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/09/ftc-announces-crackdown-deceptive-ai-claims-schemes)

## Recent changes
- European Commission adopted the EU-US Data Privacy Framework adequacy decision, restoring a legal basis (under GDPR Art 45) for EU-to-US personal data transfers after the Privacy Shield was invalidated in Schrems II. US companies join by self-certifying with the Department of Commerce; FTC enforces commitments; new US safeguards (EO 14086: necessary/proportionate intelligence access + Data Protection Review Court) underpin adequacy. First periodic review within a year of entry into force. (10 July 2023)  [source](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/api/files/document/print/en/ip_23_3721/IP_23_3721_EN.pdf)
- President Biden signed EO 14086, imposing necessity and proportionality limits on US signals-intelligence access to personal data and creating a Data Protection Review Court (DPRC) with binding remedial powers for qualifying complaints from EU individuals. This US-side legal change was the prerequisite the European Commission relied on to adopt the DPF adequacy decision, and also supports SCC/BCR transfers by improving the third-country-law assessment under Schrems II. (7 October 2022)  [source](https://federalregister.gov/documents/2022/10/14/2022-22531/enhancing-safeguards-for-united-states-signals-intelligence-activities)
- EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689, adopted 13 June 2024) began phased application. From 2 Feb 2025 the prohibited AI practices (Art 5) and general provisions apply; from 2 Aug 2025 obligations for general-purpose AI model providers, governance, and penalties apply; and from 2 Aug 2026 the high-risk AI system obligations (risk management, data governance, transparency, human oversight, logging) apply. The Act is complementary to (not a replacement for) the GDPR. A US SaaS company placing AI systems on the EU market must comply with these tiered duties. (2 February 2025 (prohibited AI practices & general provisions apply))  [source](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT?uri=CELEX%3A32024R1689)
- The FTC launched 'Operation AI Comply', announcing five Section 5 enforcement actions against companies using AI hype or selling AI tools in deceptive/unfair ways (including DoNotPay, marketed as 'the world's first robot lawyer'). Signals that there is 'no AI exemption from the laws on the books' — a US SaaS company marketing AI features must ensure its privacy/AI claims are not deceptive, on top of its GDPR/ePrivacy/EU AI Act obligations. (25 September 2024)  [source](https://ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/09/ftc-announces-crackdown-deceptive-ai-claims-schemes)
